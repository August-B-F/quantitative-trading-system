# Post-Publication Anomaly Decay

**Citation:** McLean, R.D. and Pontiff, J. (2016). "Does Academic Research Destroy Stock Return Predictability?" *Journal of Finance*, 71(1), 5-32.

**Core mechanism:** After a stock market anomaly is published, its returns decline significantly. Returns are 26% lower out-of-sample (statistical overfitting) and 58% lower post-publication (investor learning + arbitrage). The extra 32% decline reflects real-world trading arbitraging the signal away. Decay peaks 3-4 years after publication.

**Signal:** Not a trading signal per se, but a critical meta-framework:
- Discount any published anomaly's backtest returns by ~50-60% for realistic forward expectations
- Anomalies with higher post-publication trading volume increases decay faster
- Mispricing-based anomalies decay more than risk-based anomalies

**Data needed:** N/A (meta-research informing strategy design).

**Performance:** Studied 97 anomalies across publication dates from 1970s-2012. Robust across definitions.

**Weaknesses:** The 97 anomalies are primarily equity cross-sectional factors, not asset-class rotation signals. ETF rotation signals may decay differently.

**Assessment:** Definitive paper. The 58% decline is the benchmark every quant should use. **Must-know for realistic performance expectations.**

**Application to 8-ETF rotation:** Apply systematic "publication discount" to all signals. If a momentum variant shows 12% backtest return based on well-known published strategy, expect ~5-6% forward. Prefer signals harder to implement (multi-step, judgment-based) as these face higher barriers and decay more slowly.
