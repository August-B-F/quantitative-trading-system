# Excess Bond Premium (EBP)

**Citation:** Gilchrist, S. and Zakrajsek, E. (2012). "Credit Spreads and Business Cycle Fluctuations." *American Economic Review*, 102(4), 1692-1720.

**Core mechanism:** Corporate credit spreads decompose into (1) expected default risk and (2) a residual "excess bond premium" capturing risk appetite of the financial sector. EBP increases foreshadow economic downturns, declining equity prices, and rising unemployment. EBP is the *only* component of credit spreads with predictive power for the real economy.

**Signal:** Rising EBP = risk-off signal, falling EBP = risk-on. When EBP is elevated (>1 std dev above mean), rotate to defensive sectors. When compressed, overweight cyclicals. 100bp increase in EBP associated with 1.5-2% decline in industrial production.

**Data needed:** The Fed publishes an updated EBP series monthly, available since 1973.

**Performance:** Strongest single predictor of recession risk among credit market variables over four decades. Published in AER (top journal). 2000+ citations.

**Weaknesses:** Slow-moving signal -- better as regime indicator than timing signal. Does not directly tell which sectors to overweight. Using Fed series introduces publication lag.

**Assessment:** **Genuine, well-validated macro regime signal** with clear economic mechanism (financial intermediary health). Not overfit. Real alpha.

**Application to 8-ETF rotation:** Use EBP level and change as a regime overlay. Rising EBP sharply: tilt toward defensive ETFs, reduce equity exposure. Falling/compressed EBP: tilt toward cyclical/risk-on ETFs. Genuinely additive -- none of the existing signals capture credit intermediary risk appetite.
