# Baltic Dry Index as Predictor

**Citation:** Bakshi, G., Panayotov, G., and Skoulakis, G. (2011). "The Baltic Dry Index as a Predictor of Global Stock Returns, Commodity Returns, and Global Economic Activity." SSRN 1747345.

**Core mechanism:** The BDI reflects real-time global shipping demand for dry bulk commodities. Shipping rates are set in a competitive spot market with inelastic short-run supply, so the BDI captures genuine shifts in global industrial activity before they appear in official statistics.

**Signal:** 3-month percentage change in BDI. Positive and rising: overweight cyclical/commodity/industrial sectors. Negative and falling: shift to defensives. Out-of-sample predictive power for MSCI regional indices and G-7 stock markets.

**Data needed:** Baltic Dry Index (daily, freely available from FRED as DBDRY). Trivial to compute.

**Performance:** Statistically significant in-sample and out-of-sample. Economically significant portfolio strategies. Predictive power distinct from dividend yield, term spread, default spread. Multi-country evidence.

**Weaknesses:** BDI became much more volatile after 2007-2008 shipping capacity bubble. Heavily influenced by China's commodity imports. Primarily useful for industrial/commodity sectors.

**Assessment:** Mechanism is genuinely economic (real demand for physical shipping). Simple, free, easy to compute. **Likely real but modest alpha** in recent years as signal has become more widely known.

**Application to 8-ETF rotation:** Use 3-month BDI growth as a cyclical tilt signal. Strong BDI growth favors materials, energy, industrials, EM. Weak BDI growth favors bonds, utilities, US large-cap. Most useful as a confirming signal alongside momentum.
