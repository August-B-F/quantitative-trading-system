# Regime Detection Methods

Every regime detection method found in the academic literature, organized by approach.

---

## 1. HIDDEN MARKOV MODELS (HMM)

### 1.1 Standard Gaussian HMM
- **Method:** Observations generated by autoregression with parameters driven by discrete Markov chain. EM algorithm (Baum-Welch) for parameter estimation.
- **Regimes:** Typically 2 (bull/bear) or 3 (bull/bear/neutral)
- **Inputs:** Asset returns (univariate or multivariate)
- **Strengths:** Well-studied, probabilistic framework, gives transition probabilities
- **Weaknesses:** Too-frequent switching (115 switches in 33 years), Gaussian emission assumption, sensitive to initialization
- **Performance:** QuantStart implementation: Sharpe 0.48 vs 0.37 buy-hold, Max DD -24% vs -56%
- **Sources:** Yuan & Mitra (2019), MDPI (2020), QuantStart

### 1.2 HMM-LSTM Hybrid
- **Method:** Stage 1: HMM identifies regime labels. Stage 2: LSTM uses regime probabilities as additional features.
- **Performance:** 94.45% overall accuracy, bear state recall 88.89%
- **Strengths:** Two-stage reduces overfitting vs end-to-end
- **Weaknesses:** Two-stage prevents end-to-end optimization
- **Source:** HMM-LSTM Hybrid (2024)

### 1.3 HMM + Reinforcement Learning
- **Method:** HMM identifies regimes, RL agent adapts trading strategy per regime
- **Strengths:** Policy learns non-obvious allocation patterns
- **Weaknesses:** RL training instability, look-ahead bias risk
- **Source:** IDS 2025 Conference

## 2. MARKOV SWITCHING MODELS

### 2.1 Hamilton (1989) — Foundational
- **Method:** AR model with state-dependent parameters + latent Markov chain. ML estimation with EM.
- **Formula:** `y_t = mu_{S_t} + phi_1(y_{t-1} - mu_{S_{t-1}}) + epsilon_t`
- **Regimes:** 2 states. p(stay expansion) ~ 0.90-0.97, p(stay recession) ~ 0.75-0.90
- **Strengths:** Canonical, well-understood statistical properties
- **Weaknesses:** Fixed regime count, time-invariant transitions, slow to update

### 2.2 Bivariate RS — Ang & Bekaert (2002)
- **Method:** Joint stock-bond return model with regime-dependent parameters
- **Key finding:** Bear markets = high vol + high correlations + high rates
- **Application:** Switch between equity and bonds/cash based on regime probability
- **Source:** NBER WP 10080

### 2.3 Multivariate RS — Guidolin & Timmermann (2007)
- **Method:** Joint model for large-cap, small-cap, bond returns. 4 states selected by info criteria.
- **Regimes:** Crash (stock-bond corr -0.40), Slow Growth, Bull, Recovery (stock-bond corr 0.37)
- **Key insight:** Correlation structure changes sign between regimes
- **Application:** Allocate based on regime-conditional correlation structure

## 3. STATISTICAL JUMP MODELS (Modern Alternative to HMM) **[RECOMMENDED]**

### 3.1 Jump Model — Shu, Yu & Mulvey (2024)
- **Method:** Minimize `sum_t L(y_t, theta_{s_t}) + lambda × sum_t 1(s_t != s_{t-1})`
- **Lambda:** Controls switching frequency. Lambda=100 optimal.
- **Features:** 3 EWM downside deviations (half-lives 20, 60, 120 days) + 1 EWM return (120-day)
- **Regimes:** 2 per asset (bullish/bearish)
- **Performance vs HMM:** Return 12.55% vs 9.82%, Sharpe 0.78 vs 0.51, Turnover 0.16% vs 1.35%
- **Key advantage:** Only 14 regime switches vs 115 for HMM over 33 years
- **Walk-forward:** Refit every 6 months, 2000-day lookback
- **Failure modes:** Missed Black Monday (too rapid), delayed dot-com exit

### 3.2 Jump Model + XGBoost Forecaster — Shu et al. (2024)
- **Method:** JM for historical regime labels → XGBoost predicts future regimes
- **Features:** EWMA downside deviation, Sortino ratio (5/10/21-day) + VIX, yield curve, stock-bond corr
- **Assets tested:** 12 (US equity, intl, bonds, REIT, commodities, gold) — very close to 8-ETF system
- **Key insight:** Adds predictive power beyond regime persistence

### 3.3 Sparse Jump Model + Black-Litterman — Shu & Mulvey (2024)
- **Method:** SJM for per-factor regime → BL framework for portfolio construction
- **Features (~20):** EWMA active returns, RSI, stochastic %K, MACD, downside deviation, market beta, VIX, yield curve
- **Performance:** Information ratio 0.05→0.44, Max DD active -10.3%→-5.9%
- **Application:** Factor rotation with regime signals

## 4. THRESHOLD / TURBULENCE MODELS

### 4.1 Threshold Autoregression (TAR) — Tong (1978), Hansen (2000)
- **Method:** `y_t = (alpha_1 + beta_1 y_{t-d}) × I(q_t ≤ c) + (alpha_2 + beta_2 y_{t-d}) × I(q_t > c)`
- **Key difference from HMM:** Regime determined by OBSERVABLE variable, not latent state
- **Examples:** VIX > 25 → defensive; yield curve inverts → reduce equity
- **Strengths:** Simple, transparent, no latent state estimation
- **Weaknesses:** Threshold selection can be arbitrary, sharp transitions unrealistic

### 4.2 Mahalanobis Turbulence — Kritzman & Li (2010)
- **Method:** `d(y_t) = (y_t - mu)^T × Sigma^{-1} × (y_t - mu)`
- **Thresholds:** 70th/80th/95th percentile for turbulent/non-turbulent
- **Strengths:** Multi-asset, captures correlation breakdowns
- **Weaknesses:** Requires stable covariance, assumes normality

### 4.3 Smooth Transition Models
- **Method:** Logistic transition `G(s_t) = 1/(1 + exp(-gamma×(s_t - c)))`
- **Strengths:** Gradual regime change, reduces whipsaw
- **Weaknesses:** More parameters, harder to calibrate

## 5. UNSUPERVISED CLUSTERING METHODS

### 5.1 Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) — Two Sigma (2020)
- **Method:** Unsupervised GMM on 17 factor returns
- **Regimes:** 4 — Crisis, Steady State, Inflation, Walking on Ice
- **Inputs:** Equity, Credit, Interest Rates, Inflation, Value, Momentum, Quality, Currency, Volatility factors
- **Strengths:** Data-driven, no assumptions about regime count a priori
- **Weaknesses:** Regime labels interpretive, requires factor return data

### 5.2 K-Means on Macro Data — Oliveira et al. (2025)
- **Method:** Modified k-means with L2 + cosine distance on FRED-MD PCA
- **Regimes:** 6 — Crisis, Recovery, Expansionary, Stagflation, Pre-Recession, Reflationary
- **Inputs:** 127 FRED-MD variables → 61 PCA components
- **Strengths:** Rich macro information, directly tested on sector ETFs
- **Weaknesses:** Deterministic assignments, excludes bonds/commodities

### 5.3 Hierarchical Clustering on Covariance — Bucci & Ciciretti (2022)
- **Method:** Clustering on realized covariance matrices
- **Strengths:** Outperforms parametric approaches in turmoil periods
- **Application:** Detect when covariance structure indicates stress

### 5.4 AlphaArchitect Macro Clustering
- **Method:** Clustering on macroeconomic similarity
- **Regimes:** 4 — Growth, Inflation, Precarious, Crisis
- **Inputs:** HY spreads, inflation expectations, stock-bond correlation, yield curve slope
- **Application:** Map each regime to optimal ETF allocation

## 6. VIX-BASED REGIMES (Simplest Approach)

### 6.1 VIX Tercile
- **Method:** Rolling 252-day VIX percentile. Thresholds ~17.8 and ~23.1
- **Regimes:** Low Vol, Medium Vol, High Vol
- **SHAP findings:** Momentum dominates in Low Vol (0.342), Volatility dominates in High Vol (0.456)
- **Source:** RegimeFolio (2025)

### 6.2 VIX Level Thresholds
- **Method:** Fixed VIX thresholds (e.g., <15, 15-25, 25-35, >35)
- **Strengths:** Extremely simple, real-time, no estimation
- **Weaknesses:** Fixed thresholds may not adapt to structural changes

---

## 7. PRICE-BASED REGIME INDICATORS

### 7.1 Growth-Inflation 2×2 Model — Varadi/CSS Analytics (2025)
- **Growth:** S&P 500 vs 200-day SMA
- **Inflation:** Ratio of inflation-positive to inflation-negative sectors vs 200-day median
- **Regimes:** Goldilocks, Reflation, Stagflation, Deflation
- **Strengths:** Only uses price data, no macro lag

### 7.2 Momentum Crash State — Daniel & Moskowitz (2016)
- **Indicator:** Recent market decline + high realized volatility
- **Action:** Reduce/eliminate momentum exposure, switch to mean-reversion
- **Dynamic strategy doubles Sharpe ratio**
